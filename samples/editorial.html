On the quiet
power of restraint.

In an age that rewards noise, the most radical act a designer can perform is to remove — not add. A meditation on what we choose to leave out.
By Eleanor Vance Senior Editor
Photographs by Hiro Tanaka
12 min read · Dec 14, 2026

There is a particular kind of confidence that comes from subtraction. You see it in the architecture of Carlo Scarpa, in the late typography of Massimo Vignelli, in a Hemingway sentence that ends before you expect it to. Each of these makers understood that the empty space is not the absence of design — it is the design.

The blank page is not your enemy. It is the most honest collaborator you will ever have.

We are trained, almost from childhood, to fill. To add another section, another button, another color, another feature. More, we assume, is more. But the editors of this journal have spent a decade arguing the opposite: that the work arrives only when you stop defending it. That restraint is not minimalism — minimalism is a style. Restraint is a discipline.

What follows, across the next forty pages, is a collection of designers, writers, and architects who have made careers out of saying less. Their work, gathered here, is not a manifesto. It is an argument by example.
